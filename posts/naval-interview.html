Can a 3-Hour Naval Interview Save You?

2025-10-18Naval Ravikant, Entrepreneurship, Mindset, Cross-border E-commerce, Personal Growth

Hey, it's Raymen. Good to see you here.

After watching Naval's interview (Part 2), I have to say — when he says "happiness is being satisfied with the present moment," I initially wanted to argue.

But then I thought about it... he's actually right.

On Anxiety

Naval says anxiety is "unresolved stress accumulation." That hit hard. Who in cross-border e-commerce isn't anxious? Exchange rate fluctuations, platform policy changes, supply chain issues, IP infringement, returns... every single one of these can keep you up at night.

My previous state was: staring at backend data during the day, scrolling through competitor updates at night, and at 3 AM still wondering whether to lower prices tomorrow. The result? All the problems that were going to happen still happened. And I didn't make any extra money.

Then it hit me — there are far fewer things you can actually control than you think.

Exchange rates? Can't control them. Platform rules? Can't control them. What's going on inside a customer's head? Definitely can't control that.

So in 2025, I made myself a rule: Only solve problems you can solve. For everything else, let it go.

Sounds pretty Zen, right? But it's actually the most rational approach. If you spend all your energy anxious about things you can't control, you just end up being a "high-functioning anxiety patient."

The "Secretary Problem"

Naval mentioned this theory — spend one-third of your time exploring, then bet decisively.

In plain English: stop dipping your toes forever, or you'll end up soaked to your underwear.

In my years doing cross-border e-commerce, I've seen too many sellers who are "forever testing." Testing this category today, trying that platform tomorrow, switching suppliers the day after. Looks like hard work, but really it's just fear of making decisions.

I fell into this trap myself. In 2023 alone, I tested over 20 products, but each one was just a shallow attempt — none of them really took off. Looking back, it wasn't that the products were bad. I just didn't give any single product a real chance.

So my 2025 strategy is simple:

  • Spend 3 months screening 3-5 potential winners
  • Once selected, go all in for at least 6 months
  • After 6 months and still not working? Cut it. Don't look back.

Don't tell me "but what if I miss out?" — miss it then. Opportunity cost is more expensive than sunk cost.

On "Selfishness"

Naval says he's selfish. That's the first time I've heard a successful person say that word so openly.

But think about it — not being selfish would be weird.

Who in business isn't selfish? You say you're doing cross-border e-commerce to benefit all of humanity? Come on, you're in it to make money, right?

The problem is everyone likes to pretend. They have to attach some "mission" or "values" to making money. Like you don't deserve this era if you're not talking about dreams.

I'm pretty straightforward about it now: I want to make money. Lots of it. I'm materialistic. So what?

But that doesn't stop me from making great products, providing excellent service, and leading a solid team. Because these are all means to making more money.

Happy customers → higher repurchase rates → increased profits. Stable team → improved efficiency → reduced costs. Great products → word-of-mouth → cheaper customer acquisition.

See? Selfishness and helping others aren't contradictory at all. True selfishness is serving all stakeholders well, because that's how you make more money.

What I'm Actually Going to Do

After the interview, I wrote down some "counterintuitive" plans for myself:

1. Reduce Socializing

Naval says "default to saying no to all requests." That's brutal.

I used to think I needed to meet more people, attend more events, afraid of missing opportunities. Now I realize, 90% of socializing is useless.

In 2025, I only meet three types of people:

  • People who give me money (customers)
  • People who help me make money (partners)
  • People who make me happy (real friends)

For everyone else, a mass holiday greeting is enough.

2. Stop Learning

Don't get me wrong — not stop entirely. Stop "greedy learning."

What do I mean? Stop watching indie store tutorials today, TikTok operations tomorrow, and AI tools the day after.

Focus on one direction and master it. In 2025, I'm only studying one thing: how to increase repurchase rates.

No more courses. No more conferences. No new platforms. Just obsess over this one metric.

3. Embrace Mediocrity

This might be the most counterintuitive one.

Naval says "don't spend energy on things you can't control." I'd add: don't pursue perfection on things that don't matter either.

Spend 80% of your energy on the 20% that truly matters. For everything else, passing grade is fine.

Final Thoughts

Naval's philosophy, boiled down, is teaching you to do less.

Less is more. Slow is fast. We all know these. But how many can actually do it?

If you chase everything, you end up being "jack of all trades, master of none."

My 2025 goal is simple: become a top player in one niche.

Not big and comprehensive, but small and excellent. Not explosive growth, but steady growth. Not catching every opportunity, but catching one real opportunity.

As for Naval's talk about happiness, meditation, living in the present...

Let me make enough money first.

...

Okay fine, I admit — that's the most selfish thought of all.

But isn't that what Naval taught us?

Be honest about your desires, then pursue them rationally.

2025, let's do this. Only 100 days left.

Well... progress bar is severely behind.

Whether you're working a job or running a business, I recommend spending 3 hours listening to this interview. It won't waste your time.

Elon Musk's Shocking Secret: Why BYD, Xiaomi, and Li Auto Can Never Truly Surpass Tesla

2026-04-24Tesla, Elon Musk, EV, BYD, Xiaomi, Li Auto, First Principles, Business Strategy

Hey, it's Raymen. Thanks for reading.

When someone asked Elon Musk: "Chinese companies are 'fast learners' too. Traditional automakers have deeper pockets and longer histories. Why can't they do what you've done?"

Musk replied: "Because they practice incrementalism --- improving 5% a year, maybe 10% at best. We, on the other hand, re-examine the entire system from first principles."

Let me be blunt: Tesla has already shattered the boundaries of conventional thinking, while these manufacturers are still circling the same old playbook, tweaking 5% here, boosting 10% there, and expecting to catch up. Most people don't get it. At its core, this is about the difference between being "ruthless" and being "soft" in the game of business stratification.

Musk's distinction between "incrementalism" and "first principles" sounds like technical jargon, but it carries a deeply sociological undertone. Look at traditional automakers, BYD, and newcomers like Xiaomi and Li Auto --- they're all struggling within an old framework, patching things up with limited resources, never able to mount a cross-class challenge.

The reason isn't lack of money or inferior technology. It's that they simply can't play the ruthless game. Plus, most Chinese EV companies operate top-down. If the leadership isn't someone like Musk, Tesla's path isn't even an option for them.

Incrementalism is the classic arm-wrestling strategy --- you're both fighting on the same old map, and in the end, whoever is too afraid to fall behind and break the equilibrium first... loses.

The answer? Nobody dares.

China's EV market isn't an incremental market at all. It's a brutal zero-sum competition.

This is classic boundary maintenance in social dynamics. Just like in the workplace: ordinary employees want to break through bottlenecks, but all the rules are set by the dominant class. Unwritten rules, resource allocation, information barriers --- which one of these isn't a form of quiet oppression under a温室 (greenhouse)?

Desperately clinging to stability through tiny improvements, yet never having the guts to rewrite the rules entirely.

This isn't just a technology problem. It's a watershed moment in underlying logic.

Others are patching an old framework. Tesla is rebuilding a new one. Incremental improvement is finding paths on an old map. First principles is redrawing the map itself.

Chinese automakers aren't clueless about first principles. In fact, they've studied Tesla exhaustively. But what they focus on is sales volume. Their version of "first principles" is whoever sells the most cars is the king, whoever leads in sales is the industry frontrunner.

This isn't limited to the auto industry. In any industry's power dynamics, the strong mindset is about having the guts to cleanly dismantle all old rules, redraw the picture, and redistribute resources.

Because only by destroying the old order is there any chance of flying higher and farther.

BYD, Xiaomi, and Li Auto are playing the accumulate-and-erupt strategy. It looks steady, but it's actually self-limiting.

Here's the cruelest truth in the world: those who are afraid to abandon their past glory will never have the chance to become rule-makers.

In Musk's view, domestic carmakers are in the car-making business. But what Tesla is doing is AI and humanoid robot R&D and deployment.

This fundamental difference also brings a dimensional advantage in automotive profit models: Tesla's core profit driver is software and services, while many competitors rely on razor-thin hardware margins.

The core of this difference is a world apart in corporate positioning --- this competition is no longer a race on the same track. It's a showdown between an AI company and a manufacturing company.

BYD and Xiaomi's current primary profits still come from hardware sales, i.e., the vehicles themselves.

But in Musk's worldview, Tesla's real value was never the cars. It's FSD (Full Self-Driving) and Optimus (humanoid robot).

He made this crystal clear: "If you don't believe Tesla can solve autonomous driving, you shouldn't invest in Tesla."

In Musk's logic, future cars will be "robots on wheels." Yet in his eyes, today's competitors are still stuck viewing cars as mere transportation tools.

This tells us a brutal truth:

Only those brave enough to redraw the map deserve to win the future.

Those who fear offending others, who hesitate and waver, are destined to be marginalized.

See, this isn't just the smart EV industry's problem. It's a microcosm of society's complex conflicts. Often what's called "fast learning" and "catching up" is just putting on a softer set of shackles.

You think ruthlessness is cruelty? No. It's the necessary toughness for growth. It helps you cut weak ties, draw clear boundaries, and gain real control.

This is the core weapon in the game of business stratification. Don't just envy Musk's business miracles. First ask yourself if you have that kind of decisive, cut-the-cord grit. In this world, those who achieve great things are inevitably those who dare to swing the knife in brutal reality.

Dare to wield the blade of self-reflection. Someone too busy will never make big money.

Conversely, those who make big money are usually pretty relaxed about it.

Let me ask you one last thing: Are you willing to keep muddling through on the old map, or will you break the chains, blaze your own trail, and rewrite your own game rules?

This in-your-face "brutal choice" is the hard-hearted test every professional faces. Don't hide from it. Only those who dare to be ruthless truly avoid being crushed.

Got it? You know what I mean.

That said, Musk doesn't deny that BYD, Xiaomi, and Li Auto may surpass Tesla in sales (in fact, BYD already has in volume). That's an undeniable fact.

To sum it up in one sentence: Musk believes his competitors are racing to breed a faster horse on the same track, while Tesla is trying to build an airplane. (Switching to a different track entirely.)

Isn't the same true for us ordinary people?

Same as my real life. You're in this industry, this job, working yourself to the bone, miserable and unhappy. Step back and look at the bigger picture. The world is huge. Find something you enjoy, or at least don't hate. Stick with it. Then leave the rest to time.

That way you'll probably be better off. Because every day is unique. Every day is beautiful. Always believe that something wonderful is about to happen.

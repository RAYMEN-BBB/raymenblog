Duan Yongping's Brutal Lesson: Poor People Talk Gratitude, Rich People Talk Boundaries

2025-12-17Duan Yongping, Boundaries, Business Philosophy, Personal Growth, Entrepreneurship

Hey, it's Raymen. Good to see you here.

Recently I came across several interviews with Duan Yongping. Honestly, after watching them, I was left in silence.

This billionaire founder — the man behind Oppo, Vivo, and Pinduoduo's early investment — rarely shows up in public. But in 2025, he made a sudden, intense series of appearances: from Zhejiang University students to Xueqiu's Fang Sanwen, to Vanke's Wang Shi and his wife. One after another.

On the surface, it looks like he's "returning favors" — pouring out the wisdom he's accumulated over decades.

But if you read between the lines, he's actually doing something far more ruthless: drawing clear boundaries.

There's one detail that's particularly fascinating.

Wang Shi's wife, Ms. Tian, said she uses "physical discipline" on her daughter and thinks her husband is too indulgent. And how does the daughter react?

She rationalizes it — believes her mom hits her to "release stress."

See how clever this child is? She's using a twisted logic to justify violence, essentially people-pleasing the abuser.

Duan Yongping called this out on the spot.

He said: The most important thing in family education is a sense of security. Hitting a child destroys that security. This is absolutely unacceptable.

But then he added another blow: Letting your child hit you is also wrong. When it's time to say NO, you must say NO.

Sounds simple, right? But how many people go their entire lives without learning this?

I've met too many "nice guys" whose life stories are eerily similar:

  • Pushed around by their boss, convinced it's "for my own good"
  • Taken advantage of by friends, calling it "loyalty"
  • Emotionally blackmailed by family, labeling it "filial piety"

They swallow every grievance and call it "gratitude."

But the truth? They've never established boundaries.

Duan Yongping's concept of "Ben Fen" (doing one's part) — many people misunderstand it as being honest, kind, and non-confrontational.

Wrong.

The core of Ben Fen is: knowing what you should do and what you shouldn't; knowing what's your business and what isn't.

This isn't weakness. This is clarity.

Have you noticed a pattern? The lower you are in the hierarchy, the more you talk about relationships and favors. The higher you are, the more you talk about rules.

At poor people's gatherings, you must drink until you drop — not drinking is "disrespectful."

At rich people's social events, time is measured to the minute. When time's up, you leave. Nobody stays a second longer.

Poor people lending money are too embarrassed to ask for an IOU — afraid of hurting feelings.

Rich people negotiating deals scrutinize every clause down to the punctuation mark. After signing, they're still buddies.

This isn't coldness. This is the most basic survival wisdom for adults.

Duan Yongping keeps repeating in interviews: "Do the right thing, and do things right." Sounds like empty advice, but think about it —

What's "the right thing"? It's what aligns with your principles.

What's "doing things right"? It's executing well within your boundaries.

Kindness without boundaries is weakness. Giving without principles is people-pleasing.

His advice to young people is also quite interesting:

Find a company you like, find a boss you like, and cash in your knowledge.

Notice that word — "cash in."

Not "devote yourself," not "sacrifice." Cash in.

What does this mean? It means your relationship with the company is an exchange. You provide capability; the company gives you returns.

You don't owe anyone anything, and no one owes you anything.

He also said something incredibly practical: Strive to find something you love. If you can't, then strive to love what you're currently doing.

Sounds like motivational soup, but the logic behind it is rock-solid —

Rather than draining yourself in complaints, find a way out through acceptance.

At the end of the day, Duan Yongping's wave of "returning favors" isn't about repaying specific kindnesses.

He's repaying a debt of cognition.

Through decades of business practice, he's telling you:

True power players never roll around in the mud of human relationships. They build rules, draw boundaries, and go all-in within those boundaries.

Those who constantly talk about "gratitude" are often the ones most trapped by obligations.

And those who seem "heartless" often live the most freely.

Because they know: the scarcest resource in this world isn't money. It's energy.

Spending your energy trying to please others is the biggest waste.

So if you're the type who's "highly sensitive," "afraid of offending people," or "too embarrassed to say no," I suggest you engrave this sentence from Duan Yongping into your brain:

When it's time to say NO, say NO.

This isn't teaching you to be cold. It's teaching you self-preservation.

After all, no one will feel sorry for you because of your kindness. They'll only push further because you have no boundaries.
